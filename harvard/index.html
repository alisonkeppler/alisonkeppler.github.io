HARVARD

The problem: A team at Harvard conducts extensive research each year on faculty feedback at a wide assortment of colleges and universities, offering customized reports to participating institutions as a means of helping the administrations gauge how their schools are comparing to their competitors and across the board.* The team recently made the move to add more interactivity and development to these reports, opening the door to a range of opportunities and challenges for how users will navigate and engage with the material.

 The goal: In a freelance project with the Harvard team, work through possible user flows and report organization, analyze and provide recommendations on options for further interactivity with the provided information, and offer best practices regarding the existing structure of the report.

*Many of the specifics for this project are under NDA, so the following is kept as a high level explanation.

RESEARCH.

As the team was operating on a tight timeline, with limited days allocated for this piece of the project, research was primarily carried out via anecdotal feedback from those on the team who have worked most directly with users in the past. I was additionally able to spend some time reviewing past reports to get a sense of what information had been proven to be most valuable to the audience. Lastly, I took some time to look into similar research across higher education and other comparative studies to get a clear sense of norms and expectations in these types of complex report structures.

WIREFRAMES.

Before moving into the sketches and wireframes for new navigation, I first sat down with the team and went through some initial feedback and best practices surrounding the progress that had been made up to that point on the report. This preliminary discussion included recommendations and validation for information architecture and hierarchy, options for encouraging the desired user behavior in the face of more complicated pages, and brainstorming areas for consolidation and clarification within the content. After we’d agreed on a general direction for the next steps of working through organizational options, I went through a few iterations of navigation and user flows, identifying obstacles and problem areas in a number of different approaches. I checked in with the team on a couple different components before settling on the pros and cons for a few potential structures (some screens of which are pictured here).

DESIGN, VALIDATE & ITERATE.

While there wasn’t time allotted in this project for me to work with the developers in ultimately putting any of the preferred options to the test, we did have a final meeting in which we talked through each scenario, discussed the feasibility of each based on the team’s bandwidth and priorities, and put steps in motion to move on to the next stage of the process based on their decision to leverage some of the different features and functionality. If I had the opportunity to be involved through the final output, I would work with the team to set up post-delivery user interviews, identifying which elements of the updated report were most valuable versus any areas where they would like to see changes. I would also continue to hone in on the specific information schools need and how individuals were using the report to ensure we were able to align the report structure with user priorities across the board. Lastly, if it reached a stage of development where this would be feasible, I’d implement A/B testing on different features and navigation to identify obstacles or user preferences when interacting with the content.

Keep testing. Find a way to the solution.
